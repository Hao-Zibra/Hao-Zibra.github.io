Reliability-lens cycle-life prediction on 124 LFP cells

Early-cycle (≤100) life prediction on the public Severson et al. 2019 fleet — 124 commercial LFP/graphite cells across three build batches — read through a reliability engineer's lens: the paper's exact held-out split reproduced, then right-censored Weibull B-lives, observation-horizon censoring experiments, Weibull AFT, and SPC/EWMA drift monitoring the original paper never ran. The famous "secondary-test is harder" result turns out to be ordinary build drift visible on a control chart.

1 · The fleet

124 cells built in three batches (2017-05, 2017-06, 2018-04), each cycled to end-of-life under fast-charge protocols. Extraction was reimplemented from scratch against the raw MATLAB structs (the reference repo has no license; only its published cell-correction lists were used, cross-checked against the dataset's own notes), and the fleet life range gates at exactly [148, 2237] cycles.

PUBLIC DATA Fleet capacity fade. Discharge capacity vs cycle for all 124 cells, colored by eventual cycle life — the wide spread of lives (a ~15× range) is exactly what makes early-cycle prediction both hard and valuable.

2 · Early-cycle prediction — reproduced, then hardened

Inputs are the first 100 cycles only, on the paper's exact held-out split (41 train / 43 primary / 40 secondary test). Charge-time and policy features are excluded on purpose — they encode the label — so the numbers are honest predictions, not leakage.

Why charge-time and policy features are excluded. They are near-deterministic functions of the label (Geslin et al. 2023, Joule) — including them inflates accuracy without predictive validity. They are kept in the pipeline but hard-prefixed FORBIDDEN_ so they can never enter a model by accident. This is a data-integrity choice, and it is the difference between an honest number and a leaky one.

3 · Fleet reliability — first-of-kind on this dataset

The same 124 lives, read as reliability data: a right-censored Weibull fit (here with 0 censored — every cell reached end-of-life) with B-lives and profile-likelihood intervals. The shape parameter settles a real question — is this random failure or genuine wear-out?

4 · How early could the fleet's B10 be called?

A reliability-planning question the source paper never asks: stop the test at horizon H, right-censor every cell still alive, and see when the censored fit first locks onto the true B10. This is what a program manager needs to know before committing to a warranty number.

PUBLIC DATA B10 vs observation horizon. The censored estimate is usable from H≈500 cycles (B10 445 [327, 634], only ~33 failures observed) and converges to the full-data answer of 331 by H≈2000. Point labels show failures observed at each horizon.
PUBLIC DATA Interactive: the Weibull fit tightening as the fleet ages and more failures accrue — the confidence band narrows toward the full-data line.

5 · SPC: the drift the ML models trip over

The dataset's notorious "secondary test is harder" result has a mundane cause. Put the early-cycle features on a control chart with batch-1 as the Phase-I baseline, and the batches have simply drifted — the ML models were extrapolating across a process shift, not failing at prediction.

PUBLIC DATA Build-stability I-chart. Cycle-2 capacity and IR vs build order (struct order as a build-order proxy), against batch-1 Phase-I limits; ✗ marks out-of-limit cells. The three batches sit at visibly different levels.

6 · Within-cell early warning & degradation onset

Beyond fleet statistics, each cell carries its own early-warning signal. A within-cell EWMA on capacity flags drift long before end-of-life, and a change-point detector locates the degradation "knee" that precedes failure — both wrapped back into censored Weibull statistics.
